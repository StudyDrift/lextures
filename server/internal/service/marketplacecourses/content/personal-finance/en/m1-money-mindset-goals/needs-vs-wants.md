# Needs vs. wants

> **Educational disclaimer:** This course is for **education only**. It is **US-focused** and is **not** personalized financial, tax, investment, or legal advice. Rules and figures change — verify current details at official sources linked in each lesson. For decisions about your situation, consider a qualified professional.

A **need** is something required for health, safety, or meeting essential obligations (for many people: housing, basic food, utilities, required transportation, minimum debt payments). A **want** improves comfort or enjoyment but is optional in the short term.

## Gray areas are normal

Internet access may be a need for school or work and a want for entertainment. The point is not to shame wants — it is to **label** spending so your budget matches your priorities.

| Example | Often a need | Often a want |
|---|---|---|
| Housing | Rent or mortgage for a safe place to live | Upgrading to a larger place sooner than required |
| Food | Groceries that cover nutrition | Frequent restaurant meals |
| Transport | Getting to work/school reliably | A luxury vehicle upgrade |
| Phone | Basic service for work/safety | Latest premium device every year |

## Practice

List five recent purchases (illustrative amounts are fine). Mark each **N** (need), **W** (want), or **?** (depends). For each "?", write one sentence explaining the context.

Consumer guides from the [CFPB](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/) and lessons on [Khan Academy Personal Finance](https://www.khanacademy.org/college-careers-more/personal-finance) use similar need/want framing when teaching budgeting.
