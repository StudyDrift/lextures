# AI, ML, deep learning, and generative AI

People often say "AI" when they mean several related ideas. Separating them helps you evaluate tools and claims.

## Artificial intelligence (AI)

**Artificial intelligence** is the broad field of building systems that perform tasks that usually require human intelligence — recognizing speech, recommending a video, translating language, or planning a route. AI is an umbrella term, not one algorithm.

Everyday example: a maps app that suggests a faster route around traffic.

## Machine learning (ML)

**Machine learning** is a way to build AI systems by learning patterns from **data** instead of writing every rule by hand. You show the system many examples; it finds statistical patterns that help it make predictions on new examples.

Everyday example: a spam filter that learns which messages look like spam from past labeled email.

Which statement best distinguishes machine learning from traditional programming? In traditional programming, people write the rules. In machine learning, the system **learns rules from examples**.

## Deep learning (DL)

**Deep learning** is a kind of machine learning that uses multi-layer **neural networks**. "Deep" refers to many layers stacked together. It powers many modern image, speech, and language systems.

Everyday example: unlocking a phone with face recognition trained on many face images.

## Generative AI (GenAI)

**Generative AI** creates new content — text, images, audio, or code — that resembles patterns in its training data. Large language models (LLMs) are a common form of generative AI for text.

Everyday example: a writing assistant that drafts an email from a short instruction.

## How the terms nest

Think of nested circles: **AI** contains **ML**; **ML** contains **deep learning**; **generative AI** often uses deep learning, but not every AI system is generative.
