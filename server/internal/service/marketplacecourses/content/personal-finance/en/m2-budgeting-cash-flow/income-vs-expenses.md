# Income vs. expenses

> **Educational disclaimer:** This course is for **education only**. It is **US-focused** and is **not** personalized financial, tax, investment, or legal advice. Rules and figures change — verify current details at official sources linked in each lesson. For decisions about your situation, consider a qualified professional.

A **budget** is a plan for money coming in (**income**) and money going out (**expenses**) over a period — often one month. **Cash flow** is whether you have money left after expenses (positive) or are short (negative).

## Income

Include take-home pay (after taxes/withholding if you use net pay), side income, and regular benefits you actually receive. Be consistent: either plan with **net** (take-home) or **gross** (before deductions), but do not mix them without adjusting.

## Expenses

Group expenses so patterns are visible:

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Housing | Rent/mortgage, basic utilities |
| Food | Groceries |
| Transport | Transit, fuel, basic car costs |
| Debt payments | Minimums on loans/cards |
| Insurance | Health, auto, renters/home (as applicable) |
| Discretionary | Dining out, entertainment, subscriptions |

The [CFPB](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/) publishes consumer-friendly budgeting guidance, and [FDIC Money Smart for Adults](https://www.fdic.gov/consumer-resource-center/money-smart-adults) includes modules on spending plans. [Khan Academy Personal Finance](https://www.khanacademy.org/college-careers-more/personal-finance) also walks through income and expense tracking.
