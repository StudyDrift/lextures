# Tracking and emergency funds

> **Educational disclaimer:** This course is for **education only**. It is **US-focused** and is **not** personalized financial, tax, investment, or legal advice. Rules and figures change — verify current details at official sources linked in each lesson. For decisions about your situation, consider a qualified professional.

## Tracking cash flow

Pick a method you will actually use: a notebook, a spreadsheet, or a budgeting app. Review weekly at first. Look for:

- Expenses that quietly grow (subscriptions)
- Categories that regularly blow the plan
- Income timing mismatches (bills due before payday)

## Emergency fund

An **emergency fund** is money set aside for unexpected expenses or income interruptions — not for vacations. Common teaching targets in consumer education are a small starter buffer (for example a few hundred to about $1,000) and, over time, a larger reserve measured in months of essential expenses. The "right" size depends on your situation (job stability, dependents, insurance).

Build it gradually inside your budget's savings slice. Keep emergency savings **liquid** (easy to access) and separate from everyday spending if that helps you avoid dipping in casually.

Guidance on saving and financial shocks appears across [CFPB](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/), [MyMoney.gov](https://www.mymoney.gov/), and [FDIC Money Smart](https://www.fdic.gov/consumer-resource-center/money-smart-adults).
